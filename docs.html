01 What reconciliation actually is

One real money event shows up in several systems — and they don't always agree by themselves. A €1,000 client deposit appears at the payment provider ("we received €1,000"), in the CRM ("deposit approved"), and in cTrader ("balance credited"). Reconciliation is simply checking that they agree — and surfacing every case where they don't.

Same event. Different systems. Same truth.

When they disagree, that's a break (an exception). The dangerous ones are obvious once you see them: client paid but never credited (an angry client, money in limbo) or credited but never paid (the company just gave money away). The whole machine is five steps:

sources→match→breaks→review→report & audit

The forex vocabulary is just the skin. Underneath, it's a systems problem: pull data from multiple sources, normalize it, match it, detect the exceptions, and give finance a clean trail to resolve and audit.

The result splits every transaction into six buckets — click a bucket to filter the list:

Found in every system and the figures agree. No action.
Tolerance

Duplicate

03 What the demo does under the hood

It takes three exports — payment provider, CRM, cTrader — and runs a real matching cascade: exact reference, then a tolerant match (small fee / FX / settlement-timing differences), then a fuzzy match, and whatever's left becomes a typed exception. You get a one-screen summary, a filterable list, and a click-in view that shows all three systems side by side with the reason and a suggested next action — plus a one-click reconciliation report you can print or save as PDF, and a CSV of the full result set. Nothing is uploaded anywhere — it all runs in the browser.

Tolerances used in the demo (and set with you for the real build): rounding €0.50 · processing fee up to ~2% · FX drift ±0.15% · settlement window ±2 days.

04 The data the demo expects

So you can see exactly what's going in, the demo reads three CSV exports with these shapes (the sample set follows them — and you can preview or download the three sample files right from the demo to see the exact format). It's already tolerant of real-world mess — comma or semicolon files, European or US number formats (1.234,56 or 1,234.56), different date formats, and differently-named columns all mapped to one common model:

Payment provider
psp_txn_id, payment_ref, sender_name, account, value, ccy, type, processed_at, state
CRM / back-office
crm_ref, client_name, login, txn_type, amount, currency, value_date, fx_rate, status
cTrader balance operations
balance_id, ctid_account, operation, delta_cents, currency, server_time, comment

The match keys are the shared payment reference, then amount + account + currency + value-date when no single reference exists.

06 · The real build works the other way around

You don't adapt to the demo — the system adapts to you. In production you give us your actual exports, in whatever format your systems already produce. We map your real data, define the matching rules that fit how you reconcile today, and then optimize that process inside the system.

We don't make you change how you work. We encode how you work — and make it faster, safer, and continuous: "show us your systems, your exports, and what you check by hand today. We map the flow, define the matching rules, and build the tool that finds the mismatches."

The path is deliberately de-risked: discovery (map your real flow) → MVP on your worst stream, proving value on your own data → expand the other streams → optimize (the system keeps learning your patterns, so the breaks queue shrinks over time).
